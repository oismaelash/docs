WhatsApp is engineered to preserve the feeling of a **genuine conversation**, and its algorithm defends that feeling fiercely. What matters is far less the raw volume you send and far more **who** you send to and **how** you send it. High-volume and/or non-consensual messaging trips WhatsApp's anti-spam mechanisms — rate limits, temporary blocks, and permanent bans. The practices below reduce operational risk.

## Critical threshold: spam reports

The single most decisive signal is how often people mark your messages as spam. If roughly **~3% of interactions are reported as spam, it can mean an irreversible ban**. Each spam report weighs **exponentially more** than each positive interaction — a handful of complaints can undo thousands of good sends.

Treat any increase in complaints or reports as an operational emergency: pause campaigns, review targeting and consent, and reduce volume immediately.

## Warm up new numbers

New numbers are roughly **8x more likely to be banned immediately**, so maturing a number before automating it is the highest-leverage precaution you can take.

- **Organic warm-up** — use the number personally for several days (real two-way conversations) before any automation touches it.
- **Strategic timing** — wait **24–48h after registration** before scanning the API QR code. Preventive bans can happen just from connecting a brand-new number to automation too early.
- **Complete profile** — set photo, name, and description **before the first send**. Never reuse data (name, photo, description) from previously banned numbers.

## Decisive factors

Volume alone rarely triggers a ban. These behavioral factors do:

- **Recipient diversity** — sending to 100 **different** people is riskier than sending 1000 messages to 10 recurring contacts. Broad, cold reach looks like spam; repeated conversation with known contacts looks genuine.
- **Active double consent** — the recipient should both **save your number** _and_ **send a trigger** (e.g. "I want offers"). Presumed consent is not consent.
- **Sensitive content** — words like **"PIX"**, **"boleto"**, or **"credit card"** trigger automatic checks. Handle transactional and financial language with extra care.
- **Global context** — elections, crises, and fake-news cycles raise moderation pressure by roughly **40–60%**. Reduce volume during those periods.

## Continuous adaptation

Patterns that worked yesterday become detectable tomorrow. Keep evolving how you send:

- **Visible opt-out** — a clear "Type STOP" is your **main defense against reports**. Provide it on every campaign and **honor it immediately**. Only message contacts who gave explicit, verifiable opt-in; never buy contact lists.
- **Structural personalization** — don't just swap `{name}`. Vary the **structure** of the template itself so sends don't form a rigid, detectable pattern. For MARKETING template sends, the API's `marketingOptions.aiRewriteEnabled` flag can automatically vary the final text while preserving intent, and marketing sends receive an automatic randomized queue delay to space out pace.
- **Rotation & diversification** — don't reuse the same template for more than **7–10 days**. Mix text, media, and interactive messages instead of repeating one format.
- **A/B testing** — validate every new template with **50–100 contacts** before larger broadcasts.

## Operational summary

- **Quality \> Quantity**
- **Consent \> Presumption**
- **Adaptation \> Repetition**
- **Respect \> Conversion** — a genuine conversation is worth more than 100 ignored broadcasts.
